When Science Meets Racism

Eugenics: A Detailed Blog

Understanding how pseudoscience, racism, and prejudice shaped dark periods of human history

Overview

Are Some Humans Superior to Others?

Just like every living species, we humans are all born different. But what does it mean to be different? Are some humans better off or worse than others due to simply being different?

Nature's Lesson: The Lions and the Elephant

We all know how the law of nature works. In a jungle, there can be two different types of lions that both fight an elephant. One lion is slow but has a lot of strength and can fight off the elephant. The other lion can be very weak in strength but is very agile, so it decides to run away from the elephant. In the end, both lions survived, yet they had entirely different traits that made them get out of a similar situation.

Does that make one animal superior to another? Or more so, how does this apply to humans?

Scientific Racism: A Dangerous Belief

The belief of one human being superior to another human due to genetics is called scientific racism, and there have been many cases where scientific racism was practiced. Historically, there have been instances where certain humans were more accepted than others in society. There are a variety of reasons: looks, athletics, intelligence, and wealth.

Many people believed that intelligence, morality, criminal behavior, and several other things had a heredity factor to them.

Desirable vs. Undesirable Traits

This led to a differentiation between desired and undesirable traits, meaning people with undesirable traits such as "low" intelligence or criminal behavior should not reproduce, as they risk passing on these traits to the next generation and making society a worse place. For people with desirable traits, they were often encouraged to reproduce and were treated far better in society.

Real-World Example: Sweden (1930s-1970s)

A classical example of eugenics being implemented would be Sweden from the 1930s to 1970s, where they wanted a permanent solution to getting rid of mentally ill people. They implemented a state sterilization program targeting people that were poor, disabled, or socially undesirable.

The Dark Consequences

Although eugenics was presented as science back then, it was still based on racism, xenophobia, and misunderstanding of genetics. They would often target colored individuals. These beliefs led to:

  • Forced sterilizations
  • Immigration restrictions
  • Segregation
  • Even genocide

Nazi Germany and the Holocaust

One major example of genocide would be Nazi Germany. The Nazi regime used eugenic beliefs to justify forced sterilization, segregation, and genocide during the Holocaust. Millions of Jewish people, Roma people, disabled individuals, and LGBTQ+ individuals were persecuted because the Nazis believed they were genetically inferior.

The History of Eugenics

Francis Galton and the Birth of Eugenics (1883)

Francis Galton coined the term "eugenics" in 1883, during a time when Darwinism and natural selection were starting to gain acclaim. He believed society could improve the human population through controlled reproduction.

Core Beliefs of Eugenicists

Eugenicists believed that:

  • Intelligence and behavior were inherited based on genetics and not much from environmental factors
  • Some races and social groups were biologically superior (people of color often became targets)
  • Poverty, criminality, disability, and mental illness were mainly genetic
  • Society should prevent "unfit" people from having children to get rid of them from the population

The Misuse of Genetics

Eugenicists justified eugenics using basic Gregor Mendel genetics patterns that were initially created for animals and incorrectly applied to humans. This misapplication of simple genetic models to complex human traits was a fundamental flaw in their reasoning.

Modern Understanding Proves Them Wrong

Today, these ideas have been mostly proven wrong. While genetics can play a factor in certain aspects of life, many characteristics such as intelligence and behavior are influenced by people's environment and culture.

A person raised in poverty who has seen lots of crime is more likely to become accustomed to those crimes compared to a person who was raised in a better environment and received proper education. These potential differences in behavior are due to the environment they were exposed to rather than their genetics.

Why Eugenics is Scientifically Incorrect

The Human Genome Project (2003)

Gregor Mendel discovered basic inheritance patterns in pea plants. Eugenicists incorrectly applied these simple patterns to highly complex human traits. However, the Human Genome Project (HGP), completed in 2003, revealed surprising findings:

  • There are about 3.2 billion base pairs in the human genome
  • Humans have only about 20,000–25,000 protein-coding genes, far fewer than earlier estimates of 80,000–140,000
  • Biological complexity does not depend solely on the number of genes
  • This proved human beings are far more complex than just a few genes that may vary between populations

Simple Gregor Mendel patterns cannot be implemented for humans.

Understanding Heritability

Heritability does not mean a trait is completely determined by genes, nor does it apply to individuals. Instead, heritability measures how much variation in a trait within a population is related to genetic differences.

Environmental Factors Matter More Than We Think

Environmental factors such as education, nutrition, pollution, violence, stress, healthcare, and discrimination can strongly affect cognitive development and IQ outcomes.

The Lead Exposure Study (2022)

A major 2022 study from Duke University and Florida State University estimated that:

  • Childhood exposure to leaded gasoline between 1940 and 1996 cost the U.S. population an average of 2.6 IQ points per person in 2015
  • This totaled 824 million IQ points lost across more than 170 million Americans

This directly proves that if you come from a bad environment, it could automatically make it seem like you have lower intelligence, even though it was never a genetic factor.

Intelligence and Race: The Truth

Modern genomics shows that:

  • Intelligence involves thousands of genes plus environment
  • Race has no correlation to intelligence, or nowhere near as much as one's environment
  • Environmental and social factors are far more determinative

Conclusion

Eugenics demonstrates how science can become dangerous when combined with prejudice, racism, and political power. Although genetics can influence certain human traits, modern genomics has proven that intelligence, behavior, and human worth cannot be explained by race alone.

The Role of Environment

Environmental factors such as education, healthcare, poverty, and discrimination play major roles in shaping human outcomes.

Eugenics Today

Eugenics of some forms still exist today, as there are people in the world who are born with rare genetic disorders that can be hereditary. They can often experience discouragement or social pressure—something everyone should try their best to prevent.
